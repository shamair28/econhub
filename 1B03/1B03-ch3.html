Section 1

Why This Chapter Matters

Every price you encounter — the price of your morning coffee, your rent, the wage in your part-time job — is the outcome of supply and demand interacting in a market. Chapter 3 gives you the tools to explain those prices, predict how they change, and evaluate the consequences of any event that disrupts them. It is the single most tested chapter in introductory microeconomics, appearing in every midterm and the final exam.

The framework you build here — demand curves, supply curves, equilibrium, and shifts — is also the foundation for Chapter 4 (efficiency and government policy), Chapter 6 (elasticity), and all the firm-behaviour chapters that follow. Understanding supply and demand is not just an exam skill: it is the lens through which professional economists, business managers, and policy analysts interpret almost every economic question.

Chapter Roadmap This chapter covers: the demand curve and its shifters, the supply curve and its shifters, market equilibrium (graphical and algebraic), how equilibrium changes when curves shift, and how to classify goods (normal/inferior, substitutes/complements). All of these appear on Midterm 1.
Section 2

The Demand Curve: Consumers and Price

The quantity demanded of a good is the amount consumers are willing and able to buy at a specific price, holding all other factors constant (ceteris paribus). Quantity demanded answers the question: “At this price, how much will buyers purchase?”

The Law of Demand states that, all else equal, when the price of a good falls, quantity demanded rises; when price rises, quantity demanded falls. Two forces drive this:

  • Substitution effect: When a good becomes relatively cheaper, consumers switch toward it and away from substitutes.
  • Income effect: When price falls, consumers’ purchasing power effectively rises — the same income now buys more — so they buy more of most goods including this one.

A demand schedule lists the quantity demanded at each price. Plotting these pairs on a graph with price on the vertical axis and quantity on the horizontal gives a demand curve — which slopes downward because of the Law of Demand.

Worked Example — Reading a Demand Schedule: Premium Bottled Water
The table below shows the weekly market demand schedule for premium bottled water in a city. Draw conclusions about consumer behaviour at each price.
  Price per bottle   Quantity demanded
  ─────────────────────────────────────
       $5.00          0 million bottles
       $4.00          1 million bottles
       $3.00          2 million bottles
       $2.00          3 million bottles
       $1.00          4 million bottles

Reading the schedule:
  • At $5.00: consumers demand nothing — the price is too high
  • At $3.00: consumers demand 2 million bottles per week
  • At $1.00: consumers demand 4 million bottles — price is low enough
    that many consumers who wouldn’t buy at $3.00 now buy

Key insight: This is the MARKET demand — the sum of ALL consumers’
individual demands at each price. If one consumer buys 0 bottles at $5
but 2 bottles at $2, she contributes to the downward slope of the curve.

Slope of this demand curve:
  ΔP / ΔQd = ($5 - $1) / (0 - 4) = -$1 per million bottles
  A $1 price decrease → 1 million more bottles demanded
Plotting Convention Economists plot price on the vertical (Y) axis and quantity on the horizontal (X) axis — the opposite of the mathematical convention where the independent variable goes on X. Price is the “independent” economic variable here, but by longstanding tradition it goes on Y. Always label your axes explicitly on any exam diagram.
Section 3

What Shifts the Demand Curve

A change in price causes a movement along the demand curve — the quantity demanded changes but the curve itself stays fixed. A change in any non-price factor causes a shift of the demand curve — the entire curve moves left or right, changing the quantity demanded at every price.

This distinction is the most commonly tested concept in Chapter 3. Always ask: did the price of this good change? If yes → movement. If no → look for one of the six shifters below.

The Six Demand Shifters

1. Income

Normal goods: income rises → demand shifts RIGHT (buy more steak, electronics, gym memberships). Inferior goods: income rises → demand shifts LEFT (buy fewer instant noodles, bus tickets, generic brands). The same income change can shift demand in opposite directions depending on the good.

2. Prices of Related Goods

Substitutes (goods that serve the same purpose): if substitute’s price rises, demand for this good shifts RIGHT. If substitute’s price falls, demand shifts LEFT. Complements (goods used together): if complement’s price rises, demand for this good shifts LEFT. If complement’s price falls, demand shifts RIGHT.

3–6. Tastes, Population, Demographics, Expectations

Tastes: health trends raise demand for premium water, lower it for sugary soda. Population: more consumers → demand shifts RIGHT. Demographics: millennials buy more bottled water than older generations. Expected future price: if consumers expect prices to rise, they buy more now (shift RIGHT); if prices expected to fall, they delay purchases (shift LEFT).

Worked Example — Millennials Shift Premium Water Demand
Between 2010 and 2020, millennials became the dominant consumer age group (18–49). They are significantly more health-conscious than previous generations. How does this affect the market for premium bottled water? Identify the demand shifter and direction.
Shifter: TASTES and DEMOGRAPHICS (two shifters reinforcing each other)

Direction: DEMAND SHIFTS RIGHT
  • Millennials prefer premium, health-conscious products over
    carbonated soft drinks → taste shift
  • As millennials aged into peak spending years, they became
    a larger share of total market demand → demographic shift

Effect on demand curve:
  At every price, the quantity demanded INCREASES.
  Example: At $3.00 per bottle, demand might rise from
  2 million to 3.5 million bottles per week.

Firm response: Coca-Cola launched smartwater; PepsiCo launched
LIFEWTR; Nestlé expanded its water portfolio. This is firms
responding to a rightward demand shift — they expand capacity
because higher demand means higher profits at existing prices.

What did NOT change: the supply curve (no supply shifter here).
Only demand moved. This is a pure demand shift.

Section 4

The Supply Curve: Firms and Price

The quantity supplied is the amount a firm is willing and able to produce and sell at a specific price, all else equal. The Law of Supply states that, holding other factors constant, when price rises, quantity supplied rises; when price falls, quantity supplied falls. The logic is straightforward: higher prices make production more profitable, so firms expand output; lower prices reduce profitability, so firms cut back.

A supply schedule lists the quantity supplied at each price. Plotting these on a graph produces a supply curve that slopes upward — the opposite direction from the demand curve. The supply curve also represents the marginal cost of each additional unit: a firm will not supply the next unit unless the price covers its cost of production.

Worked Example — Reading a Supply Schedule: Premium Bottled Water
The table below shows the weekly market supply schedule for premium bottled water. What does each row tell you about firm behaviour?
  Price per bottle   Quantity supplied
  ─────────────────────────────────────
       $1.00          0 million bottles
       $2.00          1 million bottles
       $3.00          2 million bottles
       $4.00          3 million bottles
       $5.00          4 million bottles

Reading the schedule:
  • At $1.00: no firms supply — price doesn’t cover production costs
  • At $3.00: 2 million bottles supplied — profitable to produce at scale
  • At $5.00: 4 million bottles supplied — high price justifies large output

Market supply = sum of ALL firms’ individual supplies.
If a new firm enters the market (e.g., PepsiCo launches LIFEWTR),
the market supply increases — a SHIFT in the supply curve, not a
movement (the existing firms haven’t changed their behaviour at each price).

Slope of this supply curve:
  ΔP / ΔQs = ($5 - $1) / (4 - 0) = +$1 per million bottles
  A $1 price increase → 1 million more bottles supplied
Section 5

What Shifts the Supply Curve

Just as with demand, a change in the price of the good causes a movement along the supply curve. A change in a non-price factor causes a shift of the supply curve — the entire curve moves, changing the quantity supplied at every price. There are five main supply shifters:

Supply ShifterDirection of ShiftExample
Input prices fallRIGHT (supply increases)Cheaper plastic bottles → lower production cost → more water supplied at every price
Input prices riseLEFT (supply decreases)Wage increases for bottling workers → higher cost → less supplied
Technology improvesRIGHT (supply increases)New automated bottling line cuts cost per unit → more supplied at each price
Number of firms increasesRIGHT (supply increases)PepsiCo enters with LIFEWTR → total market supply rises
Expected future price risesLEFT (supply decreases today)Firms store product to sell later at higher price → less on market now
Worked Example — Technology Improvement Shifts Supply Right
Nestlé introduces a new high-speed UV purification process that cuts the cost of purifying water by 40%. Identify the supply shifter and trace through the effect on the premium water market.
Supply shifter: TECHNOLOGICAL IMPROVEMENT
Direction: Supply curve shifts RIGHT

Before the technology:
  At $3.00, Nestlé supplies 500,000 bottles/week

After the technology (same price, lower cost → more profitable):
  At $3.00, Nestlé now supplies 700,000 bottles/week
  At $2.00, Nestlé now supplies what it used to at $3.00

The entire supply curve shifts right:
  More bottles are supplied at EVERY price level.

Effect on the market (preview of Section 6):
  → At the original equilibrium price, there is now a SURPLUS
    (more supplied than demanded)
  → This surplus pushes price DOWN
  → New equilibrium: LOWER price, HIGHER quantity

Consumer benefit: cheaper premium water
Producer benefit: higher volume (but at lower price — net effect depends
on elasticity, which we study in Chapter 6)
Substitutes-in-Production (Supply Side) A “substitute in production” is a different good that uses the same inputs or production facilities. If the price of carbonated soda rises, firms shift their bottling lines from water to soda (more profitable). This means water supply shifts LEFT — even though the water price did not change. This is the supply-side equivalent of a substitute, and it often surprises students who confuse it with demand-side substitutes.
Section 6

Market Equilibrium: Where Supply Meets Demand

Market equilibrium is the price and quantity at which the quantity demanded exactly equals the quantity supplied. At equilibrium there is no shortage (no unsatisfied buyers) and no surplus (no unsold inventory). The market clears — every willing seller finds a buyer and every willing buyer finds a seller.

Markets are self-correcting: if price is above equilibrium, a surplus develops and firms cut prices until equilibrium is restored. If price is below equilibrium, a shortage develops and firms raise prices (or consumers bid prices up) until equilibrium is restored. This is why competitive markets tend to hover around equilibrium even without central coordination.

Worked Example — Finding Equilibrium Algebraically
The demand and supply for premium bottled water are given by the equations below. Find the equilibrium price and quantity. Then find the shortage or surplus at a price of $2.00.
Given:
  Quantity demanded: Qd = 10 - 2P
  Quantity supplied: Qs = 2P - 2

Step 1 — Set Qd = Qs (equilibrium condition):
  10 - 2P = 2P - 2
  12 = 4P
  P* = $3.00

Step 2 — Substitute P* back to find Q*:
  Q* = 10 - 2(3) = 10 - 6 = 4 million bottles
  Check: Qs = 2(3) - 2 = 6 - 2 = 4 ✓

Equilibrium: P* = $3.00, Q* = 4 million bottles per week

Step 3 — Shortage/surplus at P = $2.00:
  Qd = 10 - 2(2) = 6 million bottles (demanded)
  Qs = 2(2) - 2 = 2 million bottles (supplied)
  Shortage = Qd - Qs = 6 - 2 = 4 million bottles

Interpretation: At $2.00 there is a SHORTAGE of 4 million bottles.
Consumers want more than firms are willing to supply. This shortage
creates upward pressure on price until it reaches $3.00.
Graphical vs. Algebraic Equilibrium On tests you will often need both methods. For graphs: draw both curves, find the intersection, drop dashed lines to both axes, and read off P* and Q*. For algebra: set Qd = Qs, solve for P, then substitute to find Q. Always verify by checking that Qd = Qs at your answer.
Section 7

Predicting Equilibrium Changes

Once you can identify whether a demand or supply shift occurred and which direction it moved, you can predict what happens to equilibrium price and quantity using two rules:

  • Demand shifts right (demand increases): at the old price there is now a shortage → price rises → P* rises, Q* rises.
  • Demand shifts left (demand decreases): at the old price there is now a surplus → price falls → P* falls, Q* falls.
  • Supply shifts right (supply increases): at the old price there is now a surplus → price falls → P* falls, Q* rises.
  • Supply shifts left (supply decreases): at the old price there is now a shortage → price rises → P* rises, Q* falls.

When both curves shift simultaneously, one of the two outcomes (price or quantity) is unambiguous while the other depends on the relative magnitudes of the shifts — a result that is indeterminate without more information.

Worked Example — The Hockey Card Paradox: Jacques Plante vs. Sidney Crosby
A Jacques Plante hockey card (1950s goalie) sells for $12,000. A Sidney Crosby rookie card sells for only $5,000 — even though Crosby is far more popular today. Use supply and demand to explain this paradox.
Intuition trap: “More popular → higher price” — this ignores supply.

DEMAND analysis:
  Crosby card: High demand (he’s a current superstar, many fans)
  Plante card: Lower demand (fewer people follow 1950s hockey)
  → Demand alone predicts Crosby’s card should cost MORE.

SUPPLY analysis:
  Crosby card: Extremely HIGH supply — mass-produced in the
    millions as sealed, machine-inserted foil packs. Cards are
    in mint condition and plentiful.
  Plante card: Extremely LOW supply — given away free in cereal
    boxes in the 1950s, easily bent, thrown away, or lost.
    Only a handful survive in good condition.
  → Supply alone predicts Plante’s card should cost MORE.

EQUILIBRIUM:
  Crosby: High demand ↑ but VERY high supply ↑↑ → LOW price ($5,000)
  Plante: Lower demand but EXTREMELY low supply ↓↓ → HIGH price ($12,000)

Lesson: Price is determined by BOTH supply and demand at
equilibrium — you cannot predict price from demand alone.
In the Plante case, scarcity of supply DOMINATES despite
lower demand. The supply effect was larger than the demand effect.
Section 8

Normal vs. Inferior Goods and Substitutes vs. Complements

Two classification frameworks appear repeatedly on exams. You need to apply them correctly — not just recite definitions but use them to predict which way demand shifts.

Normal vs. Inferior Goods

A normal good is one where demand increases when income rises (and falls when income falls). Most goods are normal: restaurant meals, electronics, gym memberships, premium bottled water. A inferior good is one where demand decreases when income rises. Consumers switch to better alternatives when they can afford to: instant noodles, generic-brand cereal, bus rides (vs. rideshares), and laundromats (vs. in-home washers).

“Inferior” is not a quality judgment — it just means the good has negative income elasticity. A bus ticket is perfectly functional transport; it becomes an inferior good when rising incomes let consumers switch to cars or rideshares.

Substitutes vs. Complements

Substitutes are goods that satisfy the same need and can replace each other: Coke vs. Pepsi, premium water vs. filtered tap water, bus vs. rideshare. If the price of Pepsi rises, demand for Coke increases (shift right). Complements are goods consumed together: gym memberships and premium water, coffee and cream, smartphones and phone cases. If gym membership prices rise, consumers buy fewer gym memberships — and also buy less premium water (complement demand falls, shift left).

Worked Example — Lobster: Normal or Inferior Good?
Is lobster a normal good or an inferior good? How can the same product be both?
Answer: It depends on the local context and consumer income reference point.

In Maritime Canada (Nova Scotia, New Brunswick, PEI):
  → Lobster is historically CHEAP and abundant
  → Historically served to prisoners as low-cost protein
  → As incomes rise locally, people switch to other seafood
    (salmon, scallops) and buy LESS lobster
  → In this context: INFERIOR GOOD (demand falls with income ↑)

In urban Ontario (Toronto, Ottawa) or abroad:
  → Lobster is expensive and seen as a luxury
  → As incomes rise, people afford to eat lobster for the
    first time or more often
  → In this context: NORMAL GOOD (demand rises with income ↑)

Key insight: Whether a good is normal or inferior depends on
consumers’ income level and cultural context, not on the
inherent properties of the product.

Same principle applies to: bus rides (normal at low income;
inferior once you can afford a car), instant noodles, and
many other “value” goods.
Common Classification Errors Do not assume “expensive = normal good” or “cheap = inferior good.” Classify based on the income effect only: does demand rise or fall when income increases? Also: substitutes and complements are demand-side concepts — they explain demand shifts, not supply shifts. A “substitute in production” (supply-side) is a completely different concept (Section 5).

Practice

Practice Problems

Problem 1 — Demand Schedule to Curve

A demand schedule for coffee shows: $5→100 cups/day, $4→150, $3→200, $2→250, $1→300. (a) Does this data obey the Law of Demand? (b) Write a linear demand equation in the form Qd = a − bP. (c) At what price is quantity demanded zero?

Show answer
(a) Yes — quantity rises as price falls (inverse relationship). ✓

(b) Each $1 price drop → 50 more cups demanded, so b = 50. At P=$1, Qd=300: 300 = a − 50(1) → a = 350.
Qd = 350 − 50P

(c) Set Qd = 0: 0 = 350 − 50P → P = $7.00. Quantity demanded falls to zero at a price of $7.
Problem 2 — Demand Shifters

For each event, state whether it causes a movement along the demand curve or a shift, and if a shift, which direction (left/right) and why:
(a) The price of premium water rises from $3 to $4.
(b) Consumers learn that tap water in the city contains lead.
(c) The price of gym memberships rises sharply.
(d) Household incomes fall during a recession (premium water is a normal good).

Show answer
(a) Movement along the curve (leftward). Price of THIS good changed → Qd falls. No shift.
(b) Shift RIGHT. Taste/preference change — consumers prefer premium water more at every price.
(c) Shift LEFT. Gym memberships are a complement to premium water. Higher complement price → less demand for the complementary good (water).
(d) Shift LEFT. Income falls, and premium water is a normal good → demand decreases at every price.
Problem 3 — Supply Shifters

For each event, state whether the supply of premium water shifts left or right:
(a) A drought increases the cost of sourcing spring water.
(b) A new low-energy filtration technology is introduced industry-wide.
(c) The government grants three new licences to enter the premium water market.
(d) Firms expect the price of premium water to rise significantly next month.

Show answer
(a) Shift LEFT — input cost rises → production costlier → less supplied at every price.
(b) Shift RIGHT — technological improvement → lower production cost → more supplied at every price.
(c) Shift RIGHT — more firms in the market → total market supply increases.
(d) Shift LEFT — firms hold inventory off the market now to sell at a higher price later → less supplied today.
Problem 4 — Algebraic Equilibrium

Market demand: Qd = 80 − 4P. Market supply: Qs = 6P − 20. (a) Find the equilibrium price and quantity. (b) Calculate the shortage or surplus at a price of $8. (c) If demand increases to Qd = 100 − 4P, find the new equilibrium.

Show answer
(a) Set Qd = Qs: 80 − 4P = 6P − 20 → 100 = 10P → P* = $10; Q* = 80 − 4(10) = 40 units. Check: Qs = 6(10)−20 = 40 ✓

(b) At P=$8: Qd = 80−32 = 48; Qs = 48−20 = 28. Shortage = 48 − 28 = 20 units. Price is below equilibrium → shortage → price will rise toward $10.

(c) New: 100 − 4P = 6P − 20 → 120 = 10P → P* = $12; Q* = 100 − 4(12) = 52 units. Demand increase → higher equilibrium price and quantity. ✓
Problem 5 — Economics Application: Canadian Housing Market

Between 2020 and 2022, Canadian housing prices rose dramatically in many cities. Using supply and demand analysis: (a) Identify TWO demand shifters that pushed housing demand to the right during this period. (b) Identify ONE supply shifter that pushed housing supply to the left (or limited rightward shifts). (c) Using the rules from Section 7, predict the combined effect on equilibrium price and quantity. (d) Is the direction of the price change or quantity change ambiguous given simultaneous shifts? Explain.

Show answer
(a) Two demand shifters (rightward shifts):
1. Low interest rates (2020–2021): Cheaper mortgage financing means buyers can afford more → demand for housing increases at every price level. (Income/financing effect)
2. Remote work shifting preferences: Many workers could now work from home, raising demand for larger living spaces and extending demand to suburban/rural areas previously inconvenient for commuters. (Taste/preference shift)

(b) One supply shifter (leftward or constrained):
Construction supply chain disruptions (COVID-19): Lumber prices tripled in 2021; skilled trades shortages delayed builds. The supply of new housing shifted LEFT — fewer new homes could be completed at every price level. Existing zoning regulations also prevented rapid supply expansion in desirable areas.

(c) Combined effect predictions:
Demand RIGHT + Supply LEFT → Price clearly RISES (both shifts push price up)
Quantity direction: ambiguous — demand right pushes Q up; supply left pushes Q down.

(d) Ambiguity:
The price increase is unambiguous — both a rightward demand shift and a leftward supply shift push equilibrium price upward, and they reinforce each other.
The quantity change is ambiguous — it depends on which shift was larger. If demand increased more than supply decreased, Q rose. If supply fell more sharply than demand rose, Q fell. In practice, Canadian housing transaction volumes were high in 2020–2021 (demand dominated) then fell in 2022 as rates rose.
