Section 1

Why This Chapter Matters

Chapter 1 established that scarcity forces trade-offs. Chapter 2 gives you the tools to visualize those trade-offs, measure them precisely, and use them to understand why voluntary exchange makes everyone better off. The Production Possibilities Frontier will be the first graph you draw on a test. Comparative advantage is one of the most powerful and counterintuitive results in all of economics — it shows that even if one country (or person) is better at producing everything, both parties still gain from specializing and trading.

This chapter also introduces the circular flow model — the backbone of how economists think about the economy as a system. Every transaction you make as a student, worker, or consumer fits somewhere in that diagram. By the end of this chapter, you should be able to calculate who should produce what, prove that both parties gain from trade, and explain what conditions are required for markets to function at all.

Chapter Roadmap This chapter covers the PPF (scarcity and trade-offs graphically), opportunity cost from the PPF, economic growth (PPF shifts), absolute vs. comparative advantage, gains from specialization and trade, the circular flow model, and the legal foundations of markets. All of these appear on Midterm 1 alongside Chapters 3, 4, and 6.
Section 2

The Production Possibilities Frontier

A Production Possibilities Frontier (PPF) is a curve showing the maximum combinations of two goods or services an economy can produce with its available resources and current technology. Every point on the curve represents full, efficient use of all resources. Points inside the curve are attainable but wasteful; points outside are currently unattainable.

The PPF makes three key ideas from Chapter 1 concrete:

  • Scarcity: The curve itself is the constraint — you cannot simply produce unlimited quantities of both goods.
  • Trade-offs: Moving along the curve means producing more of one good requires producing less of the other.
  • Opportunity cost: The slope of the PPF at any point tells you exactly how much of one good you must give up to get one more unit of the other.
Worked Example — Reading a PPF Table: Cars vs. Medical Operations
An economy can produce cars and medical operations. The table below shows five efficient production combinations. Use it to answer the questions that follow.
  Point    Cars (thousands)    Operations (thousands)
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
    A            0                     10.0
    B            4                      9.4
    C            8                      7.5
    D           12                      5.0
    E           16                      0.0

Additional observations:
  • Point F (6 cars, 5 ops): lies INSIDE the frontier
  • Point G (14 cars, 6 ops): lies OUTSIDE the frontier

Questions:
  1. Which points are productively efficient?  → A, B, C, D, E (on the PPF)
  2. Which point indicates unused resources?   → F (inside the PPF)
  3. Which point is currently unattainable?    → G (outside the PPF)
  4. What could move G to become attainable?   → Economic growth (more
     resources or better technology shifts the PPF outward)
Drawing PPFs on Tests Always label both axes with specific goods (not just “Good X”). Mark at least one efficient point on the curve, one inefficient point inside, and one unattainable point outside. The curve should bow outward — a straight line implies constant opportunity costs, which is a special case the exam will usually specify explicitly.
Section 3

Opportunity Cost and the Bowed-Out PPF

The slope of the PPF at any point equals the opportunity cost of producing one more unit of the good on the horizontal axis, measured in units of the good on the vertical axis. As you move rightward along a bowed PPF — producing more cars, say — the slope becomes steeper. This means the opportunity cost of each additional car rises. This is the principle of increasing marginal opportunity costs.

Why do opportunity costs increase? Because resources are not equally suited to producing all goods. When you start shifting resources from medical operations to cars, you first reassign workers who are only marginally better at operations — these workers adapt easily to car production. But as you push further, you must reassign specialists (surgeons, radiologists) who are much better suited to medicine. Pulling a surgeon away from the operating theatre to make cars costs far more operations per car than pulling a general administrator did. Resources are not perfectly interchangeable — this is why the PPF bows outward rather than being a straight line.

Worked Example — Calculating Opportunity Cost Along the PPF
Using the Cars vs. Operations table from Section 2, calculate the opportunity cost of producing one additional car when moving from Point A→B and from Point D→E.
Moving from A to B (0→4 cars, 10→9.4 ops):
  Cars gained:       4 thousand
  Operations lost:   0.6 thousand

  OC of 1 car (A→B) = 0.6 ÷ 4 = 0.15 thousand operations per car

Moving from D to E (12→16 cars, 5→0 ops):
  Cars gained:       4 thousand
  Operations lost:   5 thousand

  OC of 1 car (D→E) = 5 ÷ 4 = 1.25 thousand operations per car

Conclusion: OC of a car rose from 0.15 to 1.25 as production increased.
This is increasing marginal opportunity cost — the PPF bows outward.
Straight Line vs. Bowed PPF A straight-line PPF implies constant opportunity costs — every unit of one good always costs the same amount of the other, regardless of how much you produce. This only occurs when resources are perfectly interchangeable between the two goods. In practice this is rare, but textbook problems often use straight-line individual PPFs for comparative advantage calculations because the math is simpler.
Section 4

Economic Growth: Shifting the PPF

Economic growth occurs when an economy’s productive capacity increases, allowing it to produce more of both goods simultaneously. Graphically, this appears as an outward shift of the entire PPF. No point on the old frontier is any longer the maximum — the new frontier lies further out in every direction (for balanced growth) or further out along one axis (for sector-specific growth).

There are two fundamental drivers of economic growth:

  • More resources: A growing labour force, increased capital stock (more factories, machines, infrastructure), or discovery of new natural resources all expand the PPF.
  • Better technology: Improvements in the processes used to turn inputs into outputs — even without more workers or capital — allow an economy to produce more. This is why living standards can rise even without population growth.
Worked Example — Government Health Care Spending and the PPF
Canada faces a PPF trade-off between spending on health care and spending on all other government services. As the population ages, what happens to this PPF, and what is the opportunity cost of maintaining health care spending?
Scenario: Canada’s aging population increases demand for health care,
but also reduces the working-age tax base (fewer workers paying taxes).

Effect on PPF:
  • Aging reduces the labour supply → PPF shifts INWARD (contraction)
    (fewer productive workers means less can be produced overall)
  • Unless offset by productivity growth or immigration, total output falls

Opportunity cost of maintaining health care:
  → If the government holds health care at current levels as the PPF
    shrinks, it must cut other services (education, infrastructure,
    defence) proportionally more.

Policy options (each shifts or moves along the PPF):
  1. Allow immigration → larger labour force → PPF shifts back out
  2. Invest in health technology → medical services cost less per patient
     → health care PPF shifts outward on the medical axis
  3. Increase taxes → move resources from private to public sector
     (redistributes on the PPF, doesn’t shift it outward)

Key insight: An aging population is not just a social challenge —
it is literally a contraction of the economy’s production possibilities.
Section 5

Absolute vs. Comparative Advantage

Before we can understand why trade is beneficial, we need two concepts that are easily confused:

Absolute Advantage

The ability to produce more of a good using the same amount of resources as a trading partner. If your neighbour can pick 30 kg of apples per week and you can only pick 20 kg, she has an absolute advantage in apples. Absolute advantage is about productivity — who produces the most per unit of input.

Comparative Advantage

The ability to produce a good at a lower opportunity cost than a trading partner. This is the concept that actually drives specialization and trade. Even if your neighbour has an absolute advantage in both goods, it is still mutually beneficial for both of you to specialize — each in the good where your opportunity cost is lower.

The Key Rule

Trade is driven by comparative advantage, not absolute advantage. It is impossible for both parties to have a comparative advantage in the same good — by definition, one party’s OC is lower in good A, and the other’s OC is lower in good B. This guarantees mutual gains from trade always exist.

Worked Example — Apples and Cherries: Full Comparative Advantage Calculation
You can produce 20 kg apples OR 20 kg cherries per week. Your neighbour can produce 30 kg apples OR 60 kg cherries per week. Who has absolute advantage in each good? Who has comparative advantage in each good?
Step 1 — Absolute Advantage (who produces MORE?):
  Apples:   Neighbour 30 kg vs. You 20 kg → Neighbour has absolute advantage
  Cherries: Neighbour 60 kg vs. You 20 kg → Neighbour has absolute advantage
  Neighbour has absolute advantage in BOTH goods.

Step 2 — Opportunity Costs:

  YOUR opportunity costs (your PPF: 20 apples OR 20 cherries):
    OC of 1 apple   = 20 cherries ÷ 20 apples = 1 cherry per apple
    OC of 1 cherry  = 20 apples ÷ 20 cherries = 1 apple per cherry

  NEIGHBOUR’S opportunity costs (PPF: 30 apples OR 60 cherries):
    OC of 1 apple   = 60 cherries ÷ 30 apples = 2 cherries per apple
    OC of 1 cherry  = 30 apples ÷ 60 cherries = 0.5 apple per cherry

Step 3 — Comparative Advantage (who has LOWER OC?):
  Apples:   You (OC=1) vs. Neighbour (OC=2) → YOU have comparative advantage
  Cherries: You (OC=1) vs. Neighbour (OC=0.5) → NEIGHBOUR has comparative advantage

Conclusion:
  You should specialize in apples; neighbour should specialize in cherries.
  Even though the neighbour is better at BOTH, trade still benefits both parties.

Section 6

Specialization and Gains from Trade

Once you have identified comparative advantages, the next step is to show that both parties can consume more by specializing and trading than by each producing both goods independently. This is the fundamental argument for free trade — not that trade is “fair,” but that it expands what everyone can consume beyond what their individual resources allow.

The gains from trade emerge from two sources: each producer focuses resources on what they do at lowest opportunity cost (eliminating waste from misallocation), and trade allows each party to consume combinations that lie outside their own PPF. The exchange ratio (terms of trade) must fall within a range that makes both parties better off than they would be without trade.

Worked Example — Canada and the United States: Maple Syrup and Honey
Canada can produce 100 tonnes of maple syrup OR 67 tonnes of honey per year. The United States can produce 100 tonnes of maple syrup OR 100 tonnes of honey. Who has comparative advantage in each, and can both countries gain from trade?
Step 1 — Opportunity Costs:

  CANADA (100 syrup OR 67 honey):
    OC of 1 tonne syrup   = 67 ÷ 100 = 0.67 tonnes honey
    OC of 1 tonne honey   = 100 ÷ 67 = 1.49 tonnes syrup

  US (100 syrup OR 100 honey):
    OC of 1 tonne syrup   = 100 ÷ 100 = 1.00 tonne honey
    OC of 1 tonne honey   = 100 ÷ 100 = 1.00 tonne syrup

Step 2 — Comparative Advantage:
  Maple syrup: Canada OC (0.67) < US OC (1.00) → CANADA specializes in syrup
  Honey:       US OC (1.00) < Canada OC (1.49) → US specializes in honey

Step 3 — Mutually Beneficial Exchange Ratio:
  For Canada to gain: must receive more than 0.67 honey per syrup
  For US to gain:     must receive more than 1.00 syrup per honey

  A trade at 0.85 honey per tonne of syrup (or ~1.18 syrup per honey)
  satisfies both conditions — both countries gain.

Step 4 — Verify both gain:
  Canada: specializes in syrup, trades some for honey.
    Gets honey at 0.85 tonnes per tonne of syrup > its OC of 0.67 ✓
  US: specializes in honey, trades some for syrup.
    Gets syrup at 1.18 tonnes per tonne of honey > its OC of 1.00 ✓

Conclusion: Both countries consume OUTSIDE their individual PPFs
after specialization and trade.

Section 7

The Circular Flow of Income

The circular flow diagram is a model of the economy as a whole, showing how two groups — households and firms — interact continuously through two types of markets. It captures the fundamental insight that your spending is someone else’s income, and that income flows back as spending in an endless loop.

  • Factor markets are where factors of production — labour, capital, land, and entrepreneurial ability — are bought and sold. Households supply these factors; firms demand them. In return, firms pay wages, rent, interest, and profit back to households.
  • Product markets are where finished goods and services are bought and sold. Firms supply goods; households demand them. Households pay for goods using the income they earned in factor markets.
Worked Example — Tracing a Transaction Through the Circular Flow
You work 10 hours per week at a coffee shop earning $15/hour. You spend $20 of your wages at the same coffee shop buying lattes. Trace both transactions through the circular flow diagram.
Transaction 1 — You working at the coffee shop:
  WHERE: Factor Market (labour market)
  FLOW: You (Household) supply labour → Coffee Shop (Firm) demands it
  MONEY: Coffee Shop pays $150/week wages → You receive income

Transaction 2 — You buying lattes:
  WHERE: Product Market (goods and services market)
  FLOW: Coffee Shop (Firm) supplies lattes → You (Household) demand them
  MONEY: You spend $20 → Coffee Shop receives revenue

Net effect:
  $150 enters your household through the factor market.
  $20 flows back out through the product market.
  The remaining $130 flows to other product markets
  (rent, groceries, Netflix) — continuing the circular flow.

Key insight: The $20 you spend on lattes becomes part of the
coffee shop’s revenue, which pays its own workers’ wages,
which those workers spend elsewhere — the flow is continuous.
Circular Flow Limitations The basic model omits three important groups: government (taxes and spending), financial institutions (savings and investment), and the rest of the world (imports and exports). You will encounter these additions in macroeconomics (ECON 1BB3). For microeconomics, the two-sector model is sufficient to understand how households and firms are interdependent.
Section 8

What Makes Markets Work: Legal Foundations

Markets do not function automatically. For households and firms to engage in the specialization and voluntary exchange that generate gains from trade, three legal prerequisites must be in place:

  1. Property rights: The right to exclusive use and sale of your property — including physical assets (land, factories) and intangible assets (patents, brand names). Without secure property rights, no one will invest in productive assets because rivals or government can seize the returns.
  2. Contract enforcement: An independent legal system that compels parties to honour agreements. Specialization requires trusting that your trading partner will deliver what they promised. Without courts that enforce contracts, trade collapses into only immediate barter.
  3. Rule of law: A predictable, impartial legal framework. Arbitrary enforcement or corruption makes business planning impossible, discourages long-term investment, and raises the cost of every transaction.
Worked Example — South Sudan: When Legal Foundations Fail
South Sudan became the world’s newest country in 2011 but saw average income fall 65.5% between 2011 and 2016. Explain this collapse using the legal foundations framework.
South Sudan in 2011–2016:
  • Ongoing civil conflict → government unable to enforce property rights
  • Contracts not reliably honoured in court — no independent judiciary
  • Rule of law collapsed → arbitrary seizure of assets was common

Economic consequences:
  Property rights failure:
    → Entrepreneurs unwilling to invest (any profitable business can
      be seized). Easier to hold cash (even though it loses value)
      than build a factory that can be taken.

  Contract enforcement failure:
    → Specialization collapses. If a farmer can’t trust that a trader
      will pay for his grain, he won’t grow surplus crops for trade.
      Everyone reverts to subsistence production.

  Trade collapse:
    → Without specialization by comparative advantage, everyone
      produces everything themselves, very inefficiently.
      Total output plummets.

  Result: Average income fell 65.5% in 5 years — one of the
  fastest economic collapses in recorded history.

Lesson: “Free markets” require strong government support for
property rights, courts, and the rule of law to function.
Markets and legal institutions are complements, not opposites.
Common Misconception: “Free Markets Need No Government” “Free market” does not mean “no government.” It means no government restrictions on production and exchange — but it absolutely requires government to define and enforce property rights, honour contracts, and maintain the rule of law. A market without these is not a free market — it is a failed market.
Practice

Practice Problems

Problem 1 — PPF Interpretation

An economy can produce wheat (W) and steel (S). The PPF passes through points: A(0,100), B(20,96), C(40,84), D(60,60), E(80,20), F(100,0). Is point (50, 70) efficient, inefficient, or unattainable? Is point (90, 25) efficient, inefficient, or unattainable? Explain.

Show answer
Point (50, 70): Between D(60,60) and C(40,84) on the PPF. At W=50, the frontier yields about 73 units of S (interpolating). Since 70 < 73, this point is inside the PPF → inefficient (resources are being wasted or unemployed).

Point (90, 25): At W=90, between E(80,20) and F(100,0). The frontier at W=90 yields about 9 units of S. Since 25 > 9, this point lies outside the PPF → unattainable with current resources and technology.
Problem 2 — Opportunity Cost from PPF

Using the wheat/steel PPF from Problem 1, calculate: (a) the opportunity cost of producing one additional unit of wheat when moving from B→C; (b) the opportunity cost when moving from D→E. What does the change in OC tell you about the shape of the PPF?

Show answer
(a) B→C: Wheat gained = 20, Steel lost = 96−84 = 12
OC of 1 wheat = 12 ÷ 20 = 0.6 units of steel

(b) D→E: Wheat gained = 20, Steel lost = 60−20 = 40
OC of 1 wheat = 40 ÷ 20 = 2.0 units of steel

OC rose from 0.6 to 2.0 — this is increasing marginal opportunity cost, which explains why the PPF bows outward rather than being a straight line.
Problem 3 — Absolute and Comparative Advantage

Canada can produce 80 units of lumber OR 40 units of furniture per year. Sweden can produce 60 units of lumber OR 60 units of furniture. (a) Who has absolute advantage in each good? (b) Calculate all opportunity costs. (c) Who has comparative advantage in each good?

Show answer
(a) Absolute advantage:
Lumber: Canada (80 > 60) ✓   Furniture: Sweden (60 > 40) ✓

(b) Opportunity costs:
Canada: OC of 1 lumber = 40÷80 = 0.5 furniture; OC of 1 furniture = 80÷40 = 2 lumber
Sweden: OC of 1 lumber = 60÷60 = 1 furniture; OC of 1 furniture = 60÷60 = 1 lumber

(c) Comparative advantage:
Lumber: Canada OC (0.5) < Sweden OC (1.0) → Canada
Furniture: Sweden OC (1.0) < Canada OC (2.0) → Sweden
Problem 4 — Gains from Trade

Using Canada and Sweden from Problem 3: before trade, Canada produces 40 lumber + 20 furniture, Sweden produces 30 lumber + 30 furniture. After specialization, Canada produces 80 lumber (only) and Sweden produces 60 furniture (only). They trade 30 lumber for 25 furniture. Calculate each country’s consumption after trade and confirm both gain.

Show answer
Canada after trade: 80 − 30 = 50 lumber kept, + 25 furniture received
Before: 40 lumber, 20 furniture  →  After: 50 lumber, 25 furniture ✓ Gain: +10, +5

Sweden after trade: 60 − 25 = 35 furniture kept, + 30 lumber received
Before: 30 lumber, 30 furniture  →  After: 30 lumber, 35 furniture ✓ Gain: 0, +5

Both countries consume more after specialization and trade than they could produce alone — gains from trade confirmed. Both now consume outside their individual PPFs.
Problem 5 — Economics Application: Canada and the US — Wheat and Cars

Canada can produce 200 million bushels of wheat OR 10 million cars per year. The United States can produce 600 million bushels of wheat OR 60 million cars per year. (a) Calculate the opportunity cost of each good for each country. (b) Identify who has absolute advantage in each good. (c) Identify who has comparative advantage in each good and explain why. (d) Propose a mutually beneficial exchange ratio and explain why it satisfies both countries. (e) Describe what happens to each country’s consumption possibilities after specialization and trade.

Show answer
(a) Opportunity costs:
Canada: OC of 1 car = 200÷10 = 20 million bushels wheat; OC of 1 million bushels = 10÷200 = 0.05 cars
US: OC of 1 car = 600÷60 = 10 million bushels wheat; OC of 1 million bushels = 60÷600 = 0.1 cars

(b) Absolute advantage:
Wheat: US (600 > 200) ✓   Cars: US (60 > 10) ✓   → US has absolute advantage in BOTH goods.

(c) Comparative advantage:
Cars: US OC (10 bushels) < Canada OC (20 bushels) → US has comparative advantage in cars
Wheat: Canada OC (0.05 cars) < US OC (0.1 cars) → Canada has comparative advantage in wheat
Even though the US is absolutely better at both, Canada can produce wheat at half the car-opportunity-cost of the US — this is Canada’s advantage.

(d) Mutually beneficial exchange ratio:
For Canada to gain from exporting wheat: must receive more than 0.05 cars per million bushels
For US to gain from exporting cars: must receive more than 10 million bushels per car (i.e., Canada must offer >10 bushels per car)
A ratio of 0.07 cars per million bushels (≈14.3 bushels per car) lies between both OCs → both countries gain.

(e) After specialization and trade:
Canada specializes in wheat (200M bushels), trades some to US for cars. Canada can now consume wheat AND cars even though it produces no cars — consuming outside its individual PPF.
US specializes in cars (60M), trades some for Canadian wheat. US can consume both goods at quantities beyond what it could produce alone.
Both countries’ combined output and consumption potential expands: total world production is higher with specialization than without.
