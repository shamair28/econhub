Section 1

Why This Chapter Matters

Every concept you will encounter in ECON 1B03 — supply and demand, elasticity, production costs, market structures — traces back to ideas introduced in this chapter. The problem of scarcity, the logic of opportunity cost, and the discipline of marginal thinking are not just introductory vocabulary: they are the lens through which economists examine every decision, from what a household buys to how a government regulates a monopoly.

Chapter 1 also teaches you how economists think, not just what they have concluded. You will learn to distinguish facts from value judgments, understand how economic models work, and recognize that the pursuit of efficiency often involves real trade-offs with equity. These intellectual tools will pay dividends across every week of the course and into any field where clear thinking about choices and consequences matters.

Chapter Roadmap This chapter covers the three core economic ideas (rationality, incentives, marginal thinking), the concept of opportunity cost, the MB vs. MC decision rule, how economists build and test models, and the distinction between positive and normative analysis. Midterm 1 tests Chapter 1 alongside Chapters 2–4 and 6.
Section 2

Scarcity and the Economic Problem

Scarcity is the starting point of all economics: human wants are unlimited, but the resources available to fulfill them — time, money, labour, land, capital — are finite. Because we cannot have everything, every choice involves a sacrifice. This is not a pessimistic observation but a fundamental reality that shapes how individuals, firms, and governments behave.

Scarcity forces every society, regardless of its political system or level of development, to answer three fundamental questions:

  • What goods and services will be produced? With limited resources, producing more of one thing means producing less of another.
  • How will they be produced? Which combination of inputs — labour-intensive vs. capital-intensive methods — should be used?
  • Who will receive what is produced? How are goods and services distributed among members of society?

These questions must be answered whether a society uses a market economy (prices and voluntary exchange), a centrally planned economy (government directives), or a mixed economy like Canada's. The difference lies in the mechanism used to answer them — and markets tend to do so more efficiently than central planners.

Worked Example — Applying the Three Questions: Canada's Health Care System
How does Canada's publicly funded health care system answer the three fundamental economic questions?
WHAT is produced?
  → Government decides the mix: hospital beds, physician visits,
    prescription drugs, preventive care. Not every possible treatment
    is funded (e.g., many cosmetic procedures are excluded).

HOW is it produced?
  → Mix of public hospitals, private clinics, and contracted physicians.
    Provinces can set their own staffing and procurement rules.

WHO receives it?
  → Access is based on medical need, not ability to pay
    (universal coverage). But wait times mean high-need patients
    don't always receive care immediately — scarcity persists
    even in a publicly funded system.

Key takeaway: Even with universal health care, scarcity does not
disappear. Resources are still limited; choices about what to fund
and who gets treated first must still be made.
Common Misconception A trade-off does not mean you lose something — it means you forgo the next best alternative. If Canada decides to fund more MRI machines, the trade-off might be fewer long-term care beds, not "wasted" money. Every allocation of limited resources has an opportunity cost.
Section 3

The Three Key Economic Ideas

Economists distill human behaviour into three foundational assumptions that appear in every model throughout this course. Understanding these ideas deeply — not just as bullet points — will let you reason about any economic scenario from first principles.

Idea 1 — People Are Rational

Rationality in economics does not mean people are always right or never emotional. It means people make decisions they believe will make them better off, weighing costs against benefits. An economist says consumers are rational when they choose more of a good if its price drops — that predictable response to incentives is what makes economic analysis possible.

Idea 2 — People Respond to Incentives

When the costs or benefits of an action change, behaviour changes in a predictable direction. A higher wage attracts more workers; a carbon tax reduces emissions; lower interest rates stimulate borrowing. Policy analysis in economics is largely about designing or predicting how incentive changes alter behaviour — for firms, consumers, and governments alike.

Idea 3 — Optimal Decisions Are Made at the Margin

Economic decisions are rarely all-or-nothing. The relevant question is always: should I do a little more of this activity? The answer depends on the marginal benefit (MB) — extra satisfaction from one more unit — versus the marginal cost (MC) — extra cost of one more unit. If MB > MC, do more. If MC > MB, do less. Stop when MB = MC.

Worked Example — Incentives in Action: Bank Security vs. Robbery
Why don't all banks install bullet-resistant barriers ($20,000) when the average bank robbery nets only $1,200?
Rational cost-benefit analysis for a bank:

Cost of installing barrier:  $20,000 per branch
Benefit (avoided robbery loss): ~$1,200 per robbery

Unless a branch is robbed more than 16 times
(20,000 / 1,200 ≈ 16.7), the barrier costs MORE
than the losses it prevents.

Conclusion: Banks respond to financial incentives.
The "rational" choice is to absorb the robbery cost
rather than pay for expensive prevention.

Real-world nuance: Banks also consider insurance
payouts and reputational effects — but the core
logic holds: the MB of the barrier must exceed its MC.
Exam Tip Questions that ask you to explain a firm's or government's decision using "economic reasoning" almost always want you to invoke one or more of these three ideas: rational cost-benefit weighing, response to changed incentives, or marginal analysis.
Section 4

Opportunity Cost: The True Cost of Every Choice

The opportunity cost of any choice is the value of the next best alternative you give up to make that choice. This is the most important definition in Chapter 1 — and the one students most often get wrong on exams. Two critical details:

  • It is the NEXT best alternative, not any forgone option. If you choose to study economics and your next best option was working ($15/hr), the opportunity cost is $15 — not the sum of everything else you could have done.
  • It includes implicit (non-monetary) costs. If you use your savings to start a business, the opportunity cost includes both the interest you would have earned and any salary you forgave from alternative employment.

Opportunity cost explains why a doctor who could earn $400,000 per year would not work at minimum wage even if a part-time minimum-wage job is nominally "profitable." The doctor's opportunity cost makes the minimum-wage job a net loss in economic terms, even if accounting profit is positive.

Worked Example — Student Time Allocation
You have three hours on Friday evening. You can (A) study for your econ midterm, (B) work a shift earning $45, or (C) attend a free concert you value at $30. You choose to study. What is your opportunity cost?
Options ranked by value to you:
  A. Study for midterm   ← chosen
  B. Work shift          $45 value
  C. Attend concert      $30 value

Opportunity cost = value of the NEXT best forgone alternative
                 = $45 (the shift you didn't work)

Common mistake: Adding $45 + $30 = $75. WRONG.
You can only do one thing with those hours.
The opportunity cost is only the best forgone option.

Section 5

Thinking at the Margin: MB vs. MC

Most economic decisions are not binary — they involve choosing how much of something to do. The marginal framework answers this: compare the extra benefit of doing one more unit to the extra cost. This "one more unit" thinking is why economists say decisions are made at the margin.

The Marginal Decision Rule:

  • If MB > MC: do one more unit — you gain more than you give up.
  • If MB < MC: do one fewer unit — the extra cost exceeds the extra benefit.
  • If MB = MC: you are at the optimum — no improvement possible by changing quantity.

Notice that MB tends to fall as you do more of something (diminishing marginal benefit — the 5th slice of pizza is less satisfying than the 1st), while MC tends to rise (each additional unit is harder or more expensive to produce). This is why the two curves cross at a finite optimum.

Worked Example — How Many Episodes Should You Watch?
It's Sunday night before a Monday quiz. Each episode of your favourite show gives you some enjoyment (MB) but costs you study time (MC). Should you watch one more?
Episode  MB (enjoyment)   MC (lost study value)   Decision
  1         $18                 $3                 Watch (MB > MC)
  2         $15                 $5                 Watch (MB > MC)
  3         $13                 $7                 Watch (MB > MC)
  4         $10                $10                 Stop here (MB = MC)
  5          $8                $13                 Don't watch (MB < MC)

Optimal: Stop after episode 4.
Watching episode 5 would cost more (in quiz performance)
than the enjoyment you would receive.
Sunk Cost Warning When applying MB vs. MC, only consider future costs and benefits — never past (sunk) costs. If you already paid $15 for a movie ticket and are bored 30 minutes in, the ticket price is sunk. The relevant question is: is the MB of the remaining 90 minutes greater than the MC (your time)? If not, leave — the $15 is gone regardless.
Section 6

Economic Models and the Scientific Method

An economic model is a simplified representation of reality designed to isolate the key forces driving a particular behaviour or outcome. The map analogy is apt: a subway map omits buildings and topography because those details would obscure the one thing you need to know — which lines connect which stations. Similarly, economic models omit irrelevant complexity to highlight causal relationships.

Economists use the scientific method — the same process as physicists and biologists — to develop and test models. The key is that every economic theory must make a testable hypothesis: a prediction that can in principle be disproven by data. Models that consistently fail the data test are revised or abandoned.

The Five-Step Model Development Process

  1. Decide on assumptions — Simplify reality. For example, assume firms maximize profit, or that buyers have complete information.
  2. Formulate a testable hypothesis — State a specific, falsifiable prediction: "If the minimum wage rises, teen employment will fall."
  3. Use economic data to test the hypothesis — Collect real-world data and apply statistical methods to see whether the prediction holds.
  4. Revise the model if it fails to explain the data well — Weak explanatory power signals a flaw in assumptions. Adjust and re-test.
  5. Retain the revised model as a useful tool — A model that consistently explains and predicts is retained for policy analysis and forecasting.
Worked Example — Applying the Scientific Method: Minimum Wage and Teen Employment
Use the five-step process to analyze the economic model "raising minimum wage reduces teen employment."
Step 1 — Assumptions:
  • Firms hire workers only if the wage ≤ worker's productivity
  • Teens are the most price-sensitive segment of the labour market

Step 2 — Hypothesis:
  "A 10% increase in minimum wage leads to a 1–3% decrease
   in teen employment within 12 months."

Step 3 — Test with data:
  Compare teen employment rates in provinces that raised
  their minimum wage vs. those that did not (natural experiment).

Step 4 — Evaluate:
  Results are mixed: some studies show small negative effects;
  others show no effect if the local economy is growing quickly.
  Revise: add local unemployment rate as a moderating variable.

Step 5 — Revised model retained:
  "Minimum wage increases reduce teen employment primarily
   in weak labour markets; effect is muted in tight markets."
Section 7

Efficiency, Equity, and Economic Systems

Economists use two distinct concepts of efficiency. Productive efficiency means a good or service is produced at the lowest possible cost — no waste. Allocative efficiency means resources are used to produce the specific mix of goods and services that consumers value most — the right things are being made. A country can be productively efficient (low-cost production) while being allocatively inefficient (producing things nobody wants).

Equity refers to a fair distribution of economic benefits. The tension between efficiency and equity is one of the most debated issues in economic policy. Policies that redistribute income (progressive taxation, welfare transfers) may reduce incentives to work and invest, thereby reducing efficiency — but may be judged desirable on equity grounds. The efficiency-equity trade-off has no purely economic answer; it involves normative (value) judgments about fairness.

The three types of economic systems differ fundamentally in how they balance efficiency and equity, and in who answers the three fundamental questions:

Economy Type Who Decides? Productive Efficiency Allocative Efficiency Real-World Example
Market Economy Prices & voluntary exchange High — competition drives costs down High — consumers signal preferences via prices United States (largely)
Centrally Planned Government planners Low — no profit motive to cut costs Low — planners cannot know all consumer preferences USSR (historical), North Korea
Mixed Economy Markets + government regulation Moderate Moderate Canada, most of Europe

Section 8

Positive vs. Normative Analysis

Positive analysis describes the world as it is. Positive statements are factual claims that can — in principle — be tested and disproven with evidence. "The unemployment rate in Canada was 6.4% in June 2025" is positive. So is "raising the minimum wage reduces employment for teenagers." You may disagree with that claim, but it can be evaluated empirically.

Normative analysis involves value judgments about what the world ought to be. Normative statements cannot be resolved with data alone — they reflect priorities, values, and political beliefs. "The minimum wage should be raised to $20/hour" is normative. So is "Canada should adopt a universal basic income." Economists can inform normative debates by providing positive analysis (showing costs and benefits), but the normative choice ultimately belongs to citizens and policymakers.

Worked Example — Positive or Normative? Classify These Statements
Identify each statement as positive (P) or normative (N) and explain your reasoning.
1. "Canada's health care spending is 11% of GDP."
   → P. A factual claim; can be verified with government data.

2. "Canada should spend more on preventive care and less
    on acute hospital services."
   → N. "Should" signals a value judgment about priorities.

3. "A carbon tax of $50/tonne reduces emissions by ~5%."
   → P. A testable causal claim (may be right or wrong, but
     it is an empirical question).

4. "Corporations pay too little tax in Canada."
   → N. "Too little" is a value judgment about what is fair.

5. "Raising tuition by $1,000 reduces university enrollment
    by approximately 2%."
   → P. A hypothesis about cause and effect; testable with data.

6. "Post-secondary education should be free in Canada."
   → N. A policy preference based on values about equity
     and the role of government.
Value Judgment Smuggling A common pitfall: statements that sound factual but embed a normative judgment. "This policy is inefficient" seems positive, but "inefficient" often conceals a normative standard (inefficient compared to what baseline? by whose weights?). On the exam, look for loaded words: "too much," "too little," "should," "ought," "fair," "unfair," "better," "worse" — these signal normative content.
Practice

Practice Problems

Problem 1 — Opportunity Cost

Maria has Saturday afternoon free. She can (A) attend a free workshop she values at $40, (B) work a shift earning $60, or (C) see a movie she values at $25. She chooses to work. What is the opportunity cost of her decision?

Show answer
The opportunity cost is the next best alternative forgone = the workshop valued at $40. It is NOT $65 ($40 + $25), because Maria can only do one thing with her afternoon. The movie ($25) is irrelevant once the workshop ($40) is identified as the next best option.
Problem 2 — Marginal Analysis

A student can revise for exams. Each additional hour of revision has a marginal benefit (grade-improvement value) and marginal cost (fatigue): Hour 1: MB=$20, MC=$4; Hour 2: MB=$16, MC=$7; Hour 3: MB=$12, MC=$12; Hour 4: MB=$8, MC=$18. How many hours should the student revise?

Show answer
Optimal: 3 hours.
Hour 1: MB $20 > MC $4 → Study ✓
Hour 2: MB $16 > MC $7 → Study ✓
Hour 3: MB $12 = MC $12 → Stop here (MB = MC is the optimum)
Hour 4: MB $8 < MC $18 → Do not add this hour ✗
Problem 3 — Positive or Normative?

Classify each statement as Positive (P) or Normative (N):
(a) "Ontario's minimum wage is $17.20/hour."
(b) "The minimum wage should be raised to $20/hour."
(c) "A higher minimum wage reduces employment for teenagers."
(d) "Income inequality in Canada is too high."

Show answer
(a) P — Verifiable fact from legislation.
(b) N — "Should" is a value judgment.
(c) P — Empirical claim; testable with data (may be correct or incorrect).
(d) N — "Too high" embeds a value judgment about what is fair.
Problem 4 — Economic Systems

For each situation, identify the economy type and explain how the three fundamental economic questions are answered: (a) Canada's health care system; (b) Canada's restaurant industry; (c) North Korea's state-run agriculture.

Show answer
(a) Centrally planned (within health care): Government decides what is funded (what), how hospitals operate (how), and who gets care based on need (who).
(b) Market economy: Restaurants decide what to serve based on consumer demand and profit signals; prices signal who receives meals.
(c) Centrally planned: State planners dictate crops (what), collective farming methods (how), and ration distribution (who).
Problem 5 — Economics Application: Saskatchewan Minimum Wage

In 2019, Saskatchewan began raising its minimum wage from $11.06 toward $15/hour. Using the economic concepts from Chapter 1, answer: (a) Identify ONE positive claim and ONE normative claim about this policy. (b) What is the opportunity cost for a firm that keeps all current employees at the new wage? (c) Apply the marginal decision rule: at what point should a firm stop hiring minimum-wage workers?

Show answer
(a) Positive: "Raising the minimum wage from $11.06 to $15 increases labour costs for firms employing minimum-wage workers" — testable, factual.
Normative: "Saskatchewan should raise the minimum wage to improve living standards for low-income workers" — value judgment about what policy ought to be.

(b) Opportunity cost for the firm: The higher wage bill means money that could have been invested in equipment, additional workers, or returned as profit. If the firm spends an extra $50,000/year on wages, the opportunity cost is the forgone returns on that $50,000 (e.g., productivity-enhancing machinery).

(c) Marginal decision rule: A profit-maximizing firm should hire minimum-wage workers up to the point where the marginal revenue product of the last worker (extra revenue that worker generates) equals the wage of $15/hour. Once adding one more worker costs $15/hour but generates less than $15 in additional revenue, the firm stops hiring. If the minimum wage rises above what some workers produce, the firm will reduce its workforce — this is the economic basis for the concern that minimum wage increases reduce employment.
