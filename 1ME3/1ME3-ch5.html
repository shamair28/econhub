Section 1

Why This Chapter Matters

Sequences and series are the mathematical language of time. Any quantity that evolves step-by-step — a bank balance compounding each year, a firm's output growing at a steady rate, or an annuity paying a fixed sum every period — is described by a sequence. The question economists almost always ask is: what happens in the long run? That question is precisely what the theory of limits answers.

This chapter builds the toolkit you need for three recurring themes in economics: compound interest (future value of money), discounting (present value of future cash flows), and perpetuities (infinite payment streams). Beneath each of those applications sits one elegant formula — the infinite geometric series. Once you see the connection, many results in finance and macroeconomics fall into place naturally.

Big Picture Sequences and limits are also the foundation of calculus. The derivative itself is defined as a limit. Mastering limits here will make Chapter 6 (differentiation) feel like a natural extension rather than a new subject.
Section 2

Arithmetic Sequences

A sequence is simply an ordered list of numbers: u₁, u₂, u₃, …. An arithmetic sequence is one where consecutive terms differ by a fixed amount called the common difference d. Starting from a first term a = u₁, the general term is:

Arithmetic Sequence Formula

uₙ = a + (n − 1)d

where a = first term, d = common difference, n = term number.

If d > 0 the terms increase without bound (the sequence diverges to +∞). If d < 0 they decrease to −∞. Only when d = 0 is every term equal to a — a constant sequence. The key feature of arithmetic sequences is linearity: plotting uₙ against n gives points that fall exactly on a straight line with slope d.

Worked Example 1 — Finding a Term
An arithmetic sequence has first term a = 7 and common difference d = −3. Find the 12th term.
u₁₂ = a + (12 − 1)d
     = 7 + 11 × (−3)
     = 7 − 33
     = −26
Worked Example 2 — Finding d from Two Terms
In an arithmetic sequence, u₄ = 11 and u₉ = 31. Find the common difference and the first term.
u₉ − u₄ = (9 − 4)d     [difference over 5 steps]
31 − 11  = 5d
d = 20/5 = 4

u₄ = a + 3d  →  11 = a + 12  →  a = −1
Quick Check If you subtract any term from the next and always get the same number, the sequence is arithmetic and that number is d.
Section 3

Geometric Sequences

A geometric sequence has a constant ratio r between successive terms. If the first term is a, then multiplying by r each step gives:

Geometric Sequence Formula

uₙ = a · rⁿ⁻¹

where a = first term, r = common ratio (r ≠ 0), n = term number.

The value of r completely controls the long-run behaviour. When |r| < 1 the terms shrink toward zero — the sequence converges. When |r| > 1 the terms grow without bound — it diverges. When r = 1 every term equals a; when r = −1 the terms alternate between a and −a forever.

Worked Example 3 — Finding a Term
A geometric sequence has first term a = 5 and common ratio r = 2. Find the 8th term.
u₈ = 5 × 2^(8−1)
   = 5 × 2⁷
   = 5 × 128
   = 640

Convergence Rule A geometric sequence converges (its terms → 0) if and only if |r| < 1. This condition is critical — you will use it constantly when evaluating infinite sums.
Section 4

Series: Summing a Sequence

A series is the sum of the terms of a sequence. The partial sum Sₙ is the total of the first n terms: Sₙ = u₁ + u₂ + ⋯ + uₙ. Both arithmetic and geometric series have elegant closed-form formulas that avoid adding every term individually.

Arithmetic Series — Partial Sum

Sₙ = (n/2)(2a + (n − 1)d)

Equivalently: Sₙ = n · (first term + last term) / 2. Think of it as n terms times their average value.

Geometric Series — Partial Sum

Sₙ = a(1 − rⁿ) / (1 − r)  when r ≠ 1

When r = 1 every term equals a, so Sₙ = na.

Notice how differently the two series behave. An arithmetic series grows roughly like n² (quadratically), because you are adding a linearly increasing sequence. A geometric series with |r| < 1 grows quickly at first and then almost stops — the terms it is adding become negligible.

Worked Example 4 — Arithmetic Series Sum
Find the sum of the first 20 terms of the arithmetic sequence with a = 4 and d = 6.
S₂₀ = (20/2)(2×4 + 19×6)
     = 10 × (8 + 114)
     = 10 × 122
     = 1,220
Worked Example 5 — Geometric Series Sum
Find the sum of the first 10 terms of the geometric sequence with a = 3 and r = 2.
S₁₀ = 3 × (1 − 2¹⁰) / (1 − 2)
     = 3 × (1 − 1024) / (−1)
     = 3 × 1023
     = 3,069
  1. Identify whether the sequence is arithmetic (constant difference) or geometric (constant ratio)
  2. Read off a, d or r from the sequence or its formula
  3. Substitute into the correct partial-sum formula
  4. Simplify — watch for signs when r or d is negative
Section 5

Infinite Geometric Series

What happens to Sₙ = a(1 − rⁿ)/(1 − r) as n → ∞? If |r| < 1 then rⁿ → 0, so the formula collapses to a single clean expression. This is the infinite geometric series:

Infinite Geometric Series

S = a / (1 − r)  when |r| < 1

The series diverges (has no finite sum) when |r| ≥ 1.

The figure below shows partial sums Sₙ creeping toward the limit S = 2.5. Each extra term adds less and less — after about 15 terms the sum is indistinguishable from 2.5 at normal precision.

Worked Example 6 — Infinite Sum
Find the sum of the infinite geometric series: 12 + 4 + 4/3 + 4/9 + ⋯
a = 12,  r = 4/12 = 1/3     [check: |r| = 1/3 < 1 ✓]

S = a / (1 − r)
  = 12 / (1 − 1/3)
  = 12 / (2/3)
  = 12 × (3/2)
  = 18
Divergence Warning If |r| ≥ 1, the infinite sum does not exist. Always check |r| < 1 before applying S = a/(1 − r). A common mistake is applying the formula when r = 1 (every term equals a, so the sum grows to infinity) or r = −1 (the sum oscillates and never settles).
Section 6

Limits of Sequences

We have been using the word "converges" informally. A precise definition is essential for rigorous work. Intuitively, a sequence (uₙ) has limit L if uₙ gets arbitrarily close to L as n grows large. We write lim(n→∞) uₙ = L.

The formal definition makes "arbitrarily close" precise using the Greek letter ε (epsilon): for any tolerance ε > 0, no matter how tiny, all terms past some index N must lie within ε of L. The figure below visualises this — the ε-band is the shaded region, and N is where the sequence enters the band and never leaves.

Formal Definition of a Limit (ε-N)

lim(n→∞) uₙ = L means: for every ε > 0, there exists N ∈ ℕ such that |uₙ − L| < ε for all n > N.

In practice you rarely invoke the ε-N definition directly. More useful are these standard limit results:

  • lim(n→∞) 1/nᵖ = 0 for any p > 0
  • lim(n→∞) rⁿ = 0 when |r| < 1
  • Algebra of limits: limits of sums, products, and quotients obey the usual rules
Worked Example 7 — Evaluating a Limit
Find lim(n→∞) (3n² + 2n) / (5n² − 1).
Divide numerator and denominator by n²:

= lim (3 + 2/n) / (5 − 1/n²)

As n → ∞: 2/n → 0 and 1/n² → 0, so:

= (3 + 0) / (5 − 0)  =  3/5

Section 7

Economics Applications: Compound Interest & Present Value

The geometric sequence is the backbone of financial mathematics. When a principal PV grows at rate r per period, the balance after n periods is a geometric sequence: PV, PV(1+r), PV(1+r)², …. The n-th term is the future value formula:

Future Value & Present Value

FV = PV · (1 + r)ⁿ

PV = FV / (1 + r)ⁿ

r = interest (discount) rate per period, n = number of periods.

Present value asks: how much is a future cash flow worth today? A payment of $1,000 received 20 years from now is worth only ~$377 today if the discount rate is 5%. The figure shows how rapidly PV falls as the payment horizon extends.

A perpetuity pays a fixed cash flow C every period forever. Its present value is the sum of an infinite geometric series with a = C/(1+r) and r = 1/(1+r):

Perpetuity Formula

PV = C / r

C = periodic payment, r = discount rate per period. This is just the infinite geometric series S = a/(1−r) in disguise.

Worked Example 8 — Present Value
You will receive $5,000 in 8 years. The annual discount rate is 6%. What is the present value of this payment?
PV = FV / (1 + r)ⁿ
   = 5000 / (1.06)⁸
   = 5000 / 1.5938...
   ≈ $3,137.07
Worked Example 9 — Perpetuity
A bond pays $200 per year forever. The discount rate is 4%. What is the bond's present value?
PV = C / r
   = 200 / 0.04
   = $5,000

Check: this matches a(1−r)⁻¹ with a = 200/1.04, r = 1/1.04.

Section 8

Practice Problems

1

An arithmetic sequence has first term a = 10 and common difference d = −4. Find the 15th term.

Show answer
u₁₅ = 10 + (15 − 1)(−4) = 10 − 56 = −46
2

Find the sum of the first 15 terms of the geometric series with a = 1 and r = 3.

Show answer
S₁₅ = 1 × (1 − 3¹⁵)/(1 − 3) = (1 − 14,348,907)/(−2) = 14,348,906/2 = 7,174,453
3

Does the infinite geometric series 8 + 4 + 2 + 1 + ⋯ converge? If so, find its sum.

Show answer
r = 4/8 = 1/2. Since |r| = 0.5 < 1, it converges.
S = 8/(1 − 1/2) = 8/(1/2) = 16
4

Evaluate: limn→∞ (4n³ − n) / (2n³ + 7).

Show answer
Divide by n³: lim (4 − 1/n²)/(2 + 7/n³) = (4 − 0)/(2 + 0) = 2
5

Economics Application. A company expects to earn profits of $8,000 per year, starting one year from now, and these profits will continue indefinitely. A rival firm offers to buy out the company today. The relevant discount rate is 8% per year.

  1. Use the perpetuity formula to find the present value of the profit stream.
  2. If the profit is expected to last only 10 years (not forever), use the geometric series formula to find the present value. Compare with part (a).
  3. What buyout price should the company accept?
Show answer
(a) Perpetuity PV:
PV = C/r = 8000/0.08 = $100,000

(b) 10-year finite stream:
Each year's payment: PVₜ = 8000/(1.08)ᵗ, for t = 1 to 10.
This is a geometric series with a = 8000/1.08, r = 1/1.08, n = 10:
PV = (8000/1.08) × [1 − (1/1.08)¹⁰] / [1 − 1/1.08]
= 7407.41 × [1 − 0.4632] / [0.08/1.08]
= 7407.41 × 0.5368 / 0.07407
≈ $53,680

(c) The company should accept any offer above $100,000 (perpetuity) or $53,680 (10-year). The difference ($46,320) is the value of profits earned in years 11 onward.
