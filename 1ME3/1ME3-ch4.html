Section 1

Why This Chapter Matters

Chapters 1–3 dealt with linear relationships — straight lines, flat budget constraints, and constant slopes. But most economic relationships are curved. A firm's profit rises, peaks, and falls as output increases. An investment grows faster and faster over time. A population doubles, then doubles again. To model these, we need three tools: quadratic functions (for curves that peak or bottom out), exponential functions (for growth and decay), and logarithms (for undoing exponentials and measuring rates of change).

This chapter is a toolkit chapter. The techniques here — completing the square, the quadratic formula, exponent laws, log rules — will be used constantly in Chapters 6 and 7 when we start differentiating, and in optimisation problems throughout the course. Treat these as skills to drill until automatic, not just concepts to understand once.

Big picture: A monopolist's profit function is a downward-opening parabola — finding its peak is the profit maximisation problem. Compound interest is an exponential function — understanding its behaviour is the foundation of all present-value calculations. This chapter gives you the algebra to handle both.
Section 2

Quadratic Functions and Parabolas

A quadratic function has the form y = ax² + bx + c, where a, b, and c are constants and a ≠ 0. The graph of any quadratic is a parabola — a smooth, symmetric U-shaped (or ∩-shaped) curve. The coefficient a controls the direction and width of the parabola: if a > 0, the parabola opens upward and has a minimum point; if a < 0, it opens downward and has a maximum point. The larger |a| is, the narrower the parabola.

Several key features of a parabola can be read directly from the equation. The y-intercept is simply c — set x = 0 and the function value equals the constant term. The axis of symmetry is the vertical line x = −b/(2a), which passes through the vertex (the highest or lowest point). The vertex itself lies at the point (−b/(2a), f(−b/(2a))). The roots (or x-intercepts) are the values of x where y = 0 — they occur where the parabola crosses the horizontal axis.

Understanding these features lets you sketch a parabola quickly without plotting many points. Identify the direction (sign of a), mark the y-intercept, find the axis of symmetry, compute the vertex, and if the parabola crosses the x-axis, find the roots. These five steps give a complete picture of the curve's behaviour.

Worked Example
Sketch y = x² − 4x + 3 and identify the vertex, axis of symmetry, and roots.
Step 1: Direction — a = 1 > 0, so the parabola opens upward.
Step 2: y-intercept — c = 3, so the curve crosses the y-axis at (0, 3).
Step 3: Axis of symmetry — x = -b/(2a) = -(-4)/(2·1) = 2.
Step 4: Vertex — y(2) = 4 − 8 + 3 = −1. Vertex is (2, −1).
Step 5: Roots — x² − 4x + 3 = (x − 1)(x − 3) = 0 → x = 1 and x = 3.
Section 3

Finding the Vertex — Completing the Square

The vertex form of a quadratic is y = a(x − h)² + k, where (h, k) is the vertex. This form is valuable because the maximum or minimum value k and the location where it occurs x = h are immediately visible — no calculation required. Converting from standard form to vertex form is done by a technique called completing the square.

The process works as follows: factor a out from the x-squared and x terms, leaving the constant c outside. Inside the bracket, take half the coefficient of x, square it, and add and subtract that value. The added term completes the perfect square trinomial; the subtracted term (multiplied by a when brought outside) adjusts the constant. The result is the vertex form. This is not just an algebraic trick — it is the algebraic foundation for every "find the maximum" or "find the minimum" problem in the course.

When a = 1, the procedure is especially clean: add and subtract (b/2)² inside, group the perfect square, and simplify. For a ≠ 1, factor a out first, then complete the square on the expression inside the bracket, taking care to multiply the subtracted term by a when expanding back. Practicing this process until it is automatic will save significant time in optimisation problems.

Worked Example
Rewrite y = x² − 4x + 3 in vertex form by completing the square.
y = x² − 4x + 3
y = (x² − 4x + 4) − 4 + 3       ← add and subtract (−4/2)² = 4
y = (x − 2)² − 1

Vertex form: y = (x − 2)² − 1
Vertex: (h, k) = (2, −1)
Minimum value: y = −1, occurring at x = 2.

Section 4

Solving Quadratic Equations

A quadratic equation has the form ax² + bx + c = 0. There are three standard methods for solving it. Factoring works when the quadratic can be written as a product of two linear factors — fast when it works, but not always possible with integer coefficients. Completing the square always works and connects back to the vertex form developed in Section 3. The quadratic formula — derived by completing the square on the general form — always works and is the most reliable tool for numeric answers.

The quadratic formula states: x = (−b ± √(b² − 4ac)) / (2a). The expression under the square root, Δ = b² − 4ac, is called the discriminant. The discriminant tells you how many real solutions exist before you do any further computation — a powerful shortcut in economics problems where you may only need to know whether solutions exist, not their exact values.

The three cases of the discriminant cover all possibilities. When Δ > 0, the square root is a positive real number, producing two distinct roots. When Δ = 0, the square root is zero, and the single root x = −b/(2a) is exactly the x-coordinate of the vertex — the parabola just touches the x-axis at its turning point. When Δ < 0, the square root involves an imaginary number, so there are no real solutions and the parabola does not cross the x-axis at all.

Δ > 0 — Two distinct real roots

The parabola crosses the x-axis twice. The two roots are x = (−b ± √Δ) / (2a).

Δ = 0 — One repeated real root

The parabola just touches the x-axis at its vertex. The single root is x = −b / (2a).

Δ < 0 — No real roots

The parabola does not cross the x-axis. The equation has no real solutions (two complex roots).

Worked Example
Solve 2x² + 3x − 5 = 0 using the quadratic formula.
a = 2, b = 3, c = −5

Δ = b² − 4ac = 9 − 4(2)(−5) = 9 + 40 = 49

Since Δ = 49 > 0, there are two distinct real roots.

x = (−3 ± √49) / (2·2) = (−3 ± 7) / 4

x₁ = (−3 + 7) / 4 = 4/4 = 1
x₂ = (−3 − 7) / 4 = −10/4 = −2.5
Section 5

Quadratics in Economics — Profit Maximization

Many economic relationships produce quadratic functions naturally. A firm's profit function — revenue minus cost — often takes the form π(Q) = −aQ² + bQ − c, where Q is the quantity produced. The negative leading coefficient (−a, with a > 0) means the parabola opens downward, guaranteeing a maximum. This maximum exists because selling more units eventually drives down the price (under a downward-sloping demand curve) or raises marginal costs, squeezing the profit margin from both sides.

The profit-maximizing quantity Q* is at the vertex of the parabola. Using the vertex formula derived in Section 3: Q* = b/(2a). This is the quantity that maximises profit, and the maximum profit itself is π(Q*). The break-even points — where the firm just covers its costs — are the roots of π(Q) = 0. Between the two break-even quantities, the firm earns positive profit; outside them, it incurs a loss.

This structure — vertex gives the optimum, roots give the boundaries — applies broadly in economics. Revenue maximisation, consumer surplus calculations, and monopoly pricing problems all reduce to finding the vertex or roots of a quadratic. Mastering the algebra here pays dividends throughout the course and into intermediate microeconomics.

Worked Example
A firm's profit function is π(Q) = −2Q² + 120Q − 1000. Find the profit-maximizing quantity, maximum profit, and break-even points.
Profit-maximizing quantity:
Q* = −b/(2a) = −120/(2·(−2)) = −120/(−4) = 30

Maximum profit:
π(30) = −2(900) + 120(30) − 1000 = −1800 + 3600 − 1000 = 800

Break-even points (set π = 0):
−2Q² + 120Q − 1000 = 0
Q² − 60Q + 500 = 0      ← divide by −2
Q = (60 ± √(3600 − 2000)) / 2 = (60 ± √1600) / 2 = (60 ± 40) / 2
Q₁ = 10,  Q₂ = 50

The firm earns positive profit for 10 < Q < 50, with maximum profit of $800 at Q = 30.
Pattern recognition: Whenever you see a quadratic with a negative leading coefficient in economics, you are looking at a maximisation problem. The vertex gives the optimum, and the roots give the boundaries of the "profitable" region. This pattern appears in monopolist pricing, revenue maximisation, and consumer surplus calculations.
Section 6

Rules of Exponents

An exponent records repeated multiplication: xⁿ means x multiplied by itself n times. For positive integer exponents this is intuitive — x³ = x · x · x. But the definition extends naturally to zero, negative, and fractional exponents through a consistent set of rules. The key insight is that these rules are not arbitrary conventions: they are the unique extensions that keep the multiplication and division rules consistent across all real exponents.

The zero exponent x⁰ = 1 (for x ≠ 0) follows from the division rule: xⁿ / xⁿ = xⁿ⁻ⁿ = x⁰, but any nonzero number divided by itself equals 1. Negative exponents extend division: x⁻ⁿ = 1/xⁿ, so a negative exponent "flips" the base to the denominator. Fractional exponents represent roots: x^(1/n) = ⁿ√x, so x^(1/2) = √x and x^(3/2) = (√x)³. These three extensions — zero, negative, and fractional — together with the five core laws cover essentially every exponential expression you will encounter.

In economics, exponent rules appear everywhere: in Cobb-Douglas production functions (Q = K^α L^β), in power functions used to model demand elasticity, and in growth models. Simplifying these expressions quickly and correctly requires fluency with the exponent laws. Practice until the rules feel as natural as arithmetic.

Multiplication & Division

xᵐ · xⁿ = xᵐ⁺ⁿ   |   xᵐ / xⁿ = xᵐ⁻ⁿ

Powers & Roots

(xᵐ)ⁿ = xᵐⁿ   |   x^(1/n) = ⁿ√x   |   (xy)ⁿ = xⁿyⁿ

Special Cases

x⁰ = 1 (for x ≠ 0)   |   x⁻ⁿ = 1/xⁿ

Worked Example
Simplify: (3x⁴y⁻²)² · (x⁻¹y³)
(3x⁴y⁻²)² · (x⁻¹y³)
= 9x⁸y⁻⁴ · x⁻¹y³        ← power rule: (ab)ⁿ = aⁿbⁿ
= 9 · x⁸⁺⁽⁻¹⁾ · y⁻⁴⁺³    ← multiplication rule: xᵐ · xⁿ = xᵐ⁺ⁿ
= 9x⁷y⁻¹
= 9x⁷/y                  ← negative exponent: y⁻¹ = 1/y

Section 7

Logarithms — Definition and Laws

The logarithm is the inverse of exponentiation. The definition log_b(x) = y means exactly bʸ = x: "the logarithm base b of x is the power to which b must be raised to get x." Two special bases appear constantly in economics. The natural logarithm ln(x) = log_e(x) uses the base e ≈ 2.71828 — a number that arises naturally in continuous growth and differentiation. The common logarithm log₁₀(x) uses base 10 and appears in some applications, but ln is far more common in economics. Unless a different base is specified, "log" in this course usually means ln.

Three key laws govern logarithms: the product rule log(xy) = log x + log y, the quotient rule log(x/y) = log x − log y, and the power rule log(xⁿ) = n · log x. These laws convert multiplication into addition, division into subtraction, and powers into multiplication — exactly the transformations that make logarithms useful for solving exponential equations. The power rule is particularly important: it lets you bring an exponent out front and solve for it algebraically.

Logarithms and exponentials are mutual inverses: log_b(bˣ) = x and b^(log_b(x)) = x. In practice this means: applying ln to both sides of an exponential equation undoes the exponential, converting a multiplicative problem into an additive one. Similarly, exponentiating both sides of a logarithmic equation undoes the log. These two moves — "take ln of both sides" and "exponentiate both sides" — are the core tools for solving growth and interest-rate problems.

Common mistakes with logarithms: There is no rule for log(x + y) — it does NOT equal log x + log y. The log laws only apply to products, quotients, and powers. Also, log(x) is only defined for x > 0 — you cannot take the logarithm of zero or a negative number.
Worked Example
Solve for x: 3^(2x+1) = 81
Method 1 — Recognise the base:
81 = 3⁴, so 3^(2x+1) = 3⁴
Since the bases match: 2x + 1 = 4 → x = 3/2

Method 2 — Using logarithms:
ln(3^(2x+1)) = ln(81)
(2x + 1) · ln 3 = ln 81
2x + 1 = ln 81 / ln 3 = ln(3⁴) / ln 3 = 4
x = 3/2

Section 8

Exponential and Logarithmic Functions in Economics

The most direct economic application of exponential functions is compound interest. If a principal PV is invested at an annual interest rate r, compounded once per year for n years, the future value is FV = PV(1 + r)ⁿ. This formula is the cornerstone of all present-value and discounting calculations in finance and macroeconomics. The key feature is that growth is multiplicative each period, so the absolute amount added grows every year — the defining characteristic of exponential growth.

Continuous compounding is the limiting case as the compounding frequency approaches infinity. The formula simplifies to FV = PV · eʳᵗ, where r is the continuously compounded rate and t is time in years. Continuous compounding is mathematically convenient because it makes differentiation and integration straightforward, and it is widely used in theoretical finance and growth models. The difference between continuous and annual compounding is small at low interest rates but grows with both the rate and the time horizon.

Logarithms are essential for analysing growth rates. If output Y grows at a constant rate g, then Y(t) = Y₀ · eᵍᵗ, and ln(Y(t)) = ln(Y₀) + g·t — a straight line in log-time space with slope g. This is why economists routinely plot the logarithm of GDP, prices, or population: a constant growth rate appears as a straight line, making departures from trend (accelerations, recessions) visually obvious. The slope of the log-linear trend is the growth rate.

The Rule of 72: To estimate how long it takes an investment to double, divide 72 by the annual interest rate (as a percentage). At 6% interest, money doubles in roughly 72/6 = 12 years. This is a handy approximation derived from the exact formula: n = ln(2)/ln(1 + r).
Worked Example
You invest $1,000 at 5% annual interest. Compare the value after 20 years under (a) annual compounding and (b) continuous compounding.
(a) Annual compounding:
    FV = 1000 · (1.05)²⁰ = 1000 · 2.6533 = $2,653.30

(b) Continuous compounding:
    FV = 1000 · e^(0.05 × 20) = 1000 · e¹ = 1000 · 2.7183 = $2,718.28

Difference: $2,718.28 − $2,653.30 = $64.98

Continuous compounding yields about 2.4% more over 20 years at this rate.

Practice Problems

Problem 1
Completing the Square

Rewrite y = 2x² − 12x + 22 in vertex form. State the vertex and whether it is a maximum or minimum.

Show Answer 
y = 2(x² − 6x) + 22
y = 2(x² − 6x + 9 − 9) + 22
y = 2(x − 3)² − 18 + 22
y = 2(x − 3)² + 4

Vertex: (3, 4). Since a = 2 > 0, the parabola opens upward → minimum.
Problem 2
Quadratic Formula & Discriminant

For the equation 3x² − 2x + 5 = 0, compute the discriminant and determine the number of real roots. If roots exist, find them.

Show Answer 
Δ = (−2)² − 4(3)(5) = 4 − 60 = −56

Since Δ < 0, there are no real roots.
The parabola y = 3x² − 2x + 5 lies entirely above the x-axis.
Problem 3
Simplify Exponents

Simplify to a single term: (x⁶ · x⁻²) / (x³)²

Show Answer 
Numerator: x⁶ · x⁻² = x⁴
Denominator: (x³)² = x⁶
Result: x⁴ / x⁶ = x⁴⁻⁶ = x⁻² = 1/x²
Problem 4
Solve a Log Equation

Solve for x: ln(x) + ln(x − 2) = ln(15)

Show Answer 
ln(x(x − 2)) = ln(15)         ← product rule
x(x − 2) = 15                  ← since ln is one-to-one
x² − 2x − 15 = 0
(x − 5)(x + 3) = 0
x = 5 or x = −3

Since ln requires x > 0 and x − 2 > 0, we need x > 2.
x = −3 is rejected. Solution: x = 5.
Problem 5
Economics Application

A monopolist faces inverse demand P(Q) = 200 − 4Q and has total cost TC(Q) = 20Q + 100.

  • (a) Write the profit function π(Q).
  • (b) Find the profit-maximizing quantity Q* and the maximum profit.
  • (c) Find the break-even quantities.
  • (d) For what range of output does the firm earn positive profit?
Show Answer 
(a) Revenue: TR = P · Q = (200 − 4Q)Q = 200Q − 4Q²
    Profit:  π(Q) = TR − TC = 200Q − 4Q² − 20Q − 100
             π(Q) = −4Q² + 180Q − 100

(b) Q* = −180 / (2 · (−4)) = −180 / (−8) = 22.5 units
    π(22.5) = −4(506.25) + 180(22.5) − 100
            = −2025 + 4050 − 100 = $1,925

(c) −4Q² + 180Q − 100 = 0
    4Q² − 180Q + 100 = 0
    Q² − 45Q + 25 = 0
    Q = (45 ± √(2025 − 100)) / 2 = (45 ± √1925) / 2
    Q = (45 ± 43.87) / 2
    Q₁ ≈ 0.56,  Q₂ ≈ 44.44

(d) The firm earns positive profit for 0.56 < Q < 44.44.
